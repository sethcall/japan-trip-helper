Asakusa Exploration & "Dry Route" Strategy

The "Dry Route" Strategy

If it is raining heavily (or if you just want to avoid the cold), follow this modified route to minimize outdoor exposure while still seeing the best of Asakusa.

Suggested Route

Location Description
Hidden Gem: Asakusa Underground Street Google Maps Instead of walking on the street from the station, use the Asakusa Underground Street (Asakusa Chikagai). It is one of the oldest underground shopping streets in Japan and connects the Tobu Asakusa Station area towards the Shin-Nakamise area. It has a retro, Showa-era atmosphere that is an attraction in itself.
Viewpoint: Asakusa Culture Tourist Info Center Google Maps Head here (opens 9:00 AM) located directly opposite the Kaminarimon Gate. It is fully indoors. You can take the elevator to the 8th-floor observation deck, which is covered and offers a dry vantage point to look down at the temple complex and the rainy streets below.
Shopping Arcade: Shin-Nakamise Arcade Google Maps Instead of walking down the wet Nakamise-dori (the main open-air street), walk through the Shin-Nakamise arcade which is fully roofed, keeping you dry while you shop.
Culture/Museum: Edo Taito Traditional Crafts Center Google Maps Located in the nearby covered Hisago-dori arcade (opens 10:00 AM). This allows you to see authentic local crafts (woodworking, cut glass, silverware) in a museum-quality setting without getting wet.
Transport Hack: Rickshaw (Jinrikisha) Google Maps If the rain is discouraging you from walking between these spots, the Rickshaws are an excellent investment. They are equipped with canvas roofs and waterproof lap covers/blankets that wrap you up completely. The drivers know exactly which streets are covered and can drop you off right at the temple steps or restaurant entrances.

Senso-ji !

Key suggestions and tips for getting the most out of your visit to Tokyo's oldest temple.

Topic Suggestion / Tip
Ritual: The Incense Burner (Jokoro) Before entering the main hall, you'll see a large incense burner. It is customary to wave the smoke onto your body. Locals believe the smoke has healing power—wave it on your head for wisdom or on any part of your body that needs healing.
Ritual: Omikuji (Fortune Slips) Try your luck for ¥100. Shake the metal box until a stick falls out, then find the matching drawer.
Good Fortune: Keep the paper with you.
Bad Fortune: Fold the strip and tie it to the metal wires nearby to leave the bad luck behind at the temple.
Snack (Must Try): Ningyo-yaki Google Maps Small sponge cakes filled with red bean paste, baked in the shape of dolls, pagodas, or pigeons. Kimuraya on Nakamise Street is the oldest shop selling these.
Snack (Must Try): Melon Pan Google Maps A sweet, fluffy bread with a cookie-crust top (resembling a melon, but tastes like butter/sugar). Kagetsudo is the most famous shop—crispy outside, fluffy inside.
Snack (Savory): Menchi Katsu Google Maps A breaded, deep-fried ground meat patty (like a croquette). Asakusa Menchi sells juicy, piping hot ones that are perfect for a cold day.
Etiquette: Walking & Eating Important: In Japan (and especially on Nakamise Street), it is considered rude to walk while eating. Please buy your snack and stand to the side of the shop to eat it before continuing your walk.
Photo Op: Hozomon Gate & Giant Waraji Look at the back of the second large gate (Hozomon). You will see massive straw sandals (Waraji). They are crafted by villagers in Yamagata prefecture to ward off evil spirits.
