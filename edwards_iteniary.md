**Money & Payments**
*   **Cash is king:** While credit cards are accepted in many hotels and larger shops, smaller restaurants, izakayas, and local vendors often prefer cash. We used a lot of coins at temples, shrines, games, drink machines, etc.
*   **ATMs:** Not all ATMs accept foreign cards, but you can reliably use those at 7-Eleven, Lawson, & FamilyMart
*   **Currency:** The Japanese Yen (Â¥). Bring some cash upon arrival, especially for taxis, temple donations, and rural areas. You can put in a request at your home bank ahead of time with 2-3 days notice.

**Tipping Etiquette**
*   **No tipping:** Unlike in the U.S., tipping is not customary and can even be considered rude at meals, transfers, group experiences.
*   **Tipping for Private Experiences:** A cash tip is always appreciated for exceptional private guides or experiences. If you would like to leave a small tip (10-12% is fine) it needs to be handed to the guide directly in a small, clean envelope.
*   **Service charge included:** High-end restaurants or hotels may add a service fee automatically.

### Ubers and Taxis
Uber works in Japan but is limited to larger cities like Tokyo, Osaka, and Kyoto. GO and JapanTaxi are the most popular local apps, both available in English. You can use them to request rides, track your driver, and pay by credit card. Taxis also accept cash, credit cards, or IC cards like Suica and Pasmo for contactless payment.

At many major train stations or hotels in Japan, youâ€™ll notice two taxi lines:
*   **Regular Taxi Line:** These are standard taxis, and most drivers speak little or no English. Theyâ€™re perfectly fine for anyone to use. Non-Japanese travelers take them all the time. *   **Foreign-Friendly or English-Speaking Taxi Line:** Some larger stations like Tokyo Station or Kyoto Station have a separate queue for taxis with English-speaking drivers. These are fewer in number and may take longer to arrive but are great if you want easier communication.
